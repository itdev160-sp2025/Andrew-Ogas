My 3D Printers

My Introduction To 3D Printing

In 2003, while I was in highschool, a group of students were looking for suggestions for molecules to have modelled. It seemed strange that they'd need a special group for making ball and stick models of different molecules. What they told me was they were going to be made out of a powder, bound together with glue and ink in a process that would form a model layer by layer. This was my first Introduction to a process known as rapid prototyping, more commonly referred to now as 3D Printing.

After that moment, I had wanted to get involved with 3D printing. Many years later, I was able to get my own. The amazing power of being able to make 3D models on my computer and have them automatically fabricated out of a variety of plastics now sits on my desk. I've printed toys and tools, items that are purely aesthetic and items that are purely functional. What more, I now have three different printers and would like to have more. It's more than just a toy, it's a tool to adapt the world function how I want it to.

Current 3D Printers

    • 120mmx120mmx120mm build volume
    Current Upgrades:

    • Upgraded Extruder
    • Upgraded Spool Holder
    • Upgraded Hotend Fan
    Proposed Upgrades:
    • Z-axis Extension
    • Larger Build Plate
    • Full Enclosure

    Notes:
    This was my first 3D printer, and it has served me well. Despite lacking many modern features, it still works reliably. Perhaps it's simplicity had been the key to it's longevity. I've printed PLA, PETG, ABS, ASA, Nylon, and even PC(to mixed success). I probably won't do much upgrading, since I have better options now, but I do still use it.

  2. Monoprice MP Mini Deluxe SLA name: Todd Howard II: Daggerfall
    Date Acquired:
    July 2019
    Features:
    • 20 Micron Layer Resolution
    • 120mmx68mmx180mm build volume
    • 30mm/hr Print Speed
    Current Upgrades:
    • Magnetic Build Plate
    Proposed Upgrades:
    • Additional Resin Tanks
    • Air Filtration System
    Notes:
    This was my first resin 3D printer and has been quite the learning experience. It's both better and worse than filament printing. The uncured resin is toxic and gets everywhere. The software is not as straightforward as there are no common print file standards. It has in general just been a lot more trouble, despite being more simple in principle. All that being said, there is no comparison to the detail it can produce. Prints are incredibly intricate. It doesn't matter the size of the print in the X or Y dimension, only the Z dimension determines the time it will take. This makes it print remarkably fast for the size of prints it makes compared to a filament printer. I don't use it as much as I'd like, but a more convenient setup would help.
